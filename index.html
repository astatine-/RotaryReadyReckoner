What is Rotary?

Rotary is a global network of more than 1.4 million people of action in more than 46,000 clubs who share a passion for and commitment to enhancing communities and improving lives around the world. Rotary clubs exist in almost every country. Our members change lives locally and work on international projects. They take action and make a difference, and it brings them personal rewards and lifelong friendships in the process.

History of Rotary

We’ve been bringing our world closer together for over 100 years. The first Rotary club was started in Chicago, Illinois, USA, in 1905 by an attorney named Paul Harris. Harris wanted to bring together a group of professionals with different backgrounds and skills as a way to exchange ideas and form meaningful acquaintances. In August 1910, the 16 Rotary clubs then in the United States formed the National Association of Rotary Clubs, now Rotary International. Rotaract began in 1968 as a Rotary youth program. Today, there are more than 36,000 Rotary clubs and 10,000 Rotaract clubs, and they are in almost every country in the world.

Our Vision & Mission

  • Vision: Together, we see a world where people unite and take action to create lasting change — across the globe, in our communities, and in ourselves.
  • Mission: We provide service to others, promote integrity, and advance world understanding, goodwill, and peace through our fellowship of business, professional, and community leaders.

Object of Rotary

The Object of Rotary is to encourage and foster the ideal of service as a basis of worthy enterprise and, in particular, to encourage and foster:
  • FIRST: The development of acquaintance as an opportunity for service;
  • SECOND: High ethical standards in business and professions; the recognition of the worthiness of all useful occupations; and the dignifying of each Rotarian’s occupation as an opportunity to serve society;
  • THIRD: The application of the ideal of service in each Rotarian’s personal, business, and community life;
  • FOURTH: The advancement of international understanding, goodwill, and peace through a world fellowship of business and professional persons united in the ideal of service.

The Four-Way Test

From the beginning, Rotarians have emphasized the importance of acting responsibly and ethically. In 1932, The Four-Way Test was developed by Herbert Taylor to guide him in his profession. Rotary later adopted it, and it underscores Rotary’s value of integrity.
  • Of the things we think, say or do:
  • 1. Is it the TRUTH?
  • 2. Is it FAIR to all concerned?
  • 3. Will it build GOODWILL and BETTER FRIENDSHIPS?
  • 4. Will it be BENEFICIAL to all concerned?

Avenues of Service

  • Club Service: Strengthening fellowship and effective club functioning.
  • Vocational Service: Upholding high ethical standards in professions, serving through vocations.
  • Community Service: Improving quality of life for those in your locality.
  • International Service: Advancing world understanding and peace through global humanitarian efforts.
  • Youth Service: Youth development activities, leadership programs (Interact,Rotaract,RYLA, Youth Exchange).

Core Values

  • Service: Putting others first.
  • Fellowship: Building lasting friendships.
  • Diversity: Embracing all backgrounds, cultures, and perspectives.
  • Integrity: Upholding high ethical standards (The Four-Way Test).
  • Leadership: Applying skills to solve real-world problems.

How Rotary is Structured

  • Members: Individual Rotarians, the heart of Rotary.
  • Clubs: Autonomous local units (93+ in D3192). Organize projects, meet regularly.
  • Districts: Groups of clubs (e.g., RI District 3192). Led by a District Governor. Provide support and facilitate larger projects.
  • Zones: Geographical groupings of districts.
  • Rotary International (RI): The global association, led by the RI President and Board of Directors. Provides global framework and support.

RI's Areas of Focus

  • Promoting Peace: Resolving conflict, fostering understanding.
  • Fighting Disease: Health education, disease prevention, healthcare access (e.g., PolioPlus).
  • Providing Clean Water, Sanitation, and Hygiene: Water solutions, hygiene education.
  • Saving Mothers and Children: Maternal & infant health, reducing mortality.
  • Supporting Education: Literacy, basic education, vocational training.
  • Growing Local Economies: Community economic development.
  • Protecting the Environment: Environmental sustainability, resource management.

Major Global Initiatives

  • PolioPlus: Rotary's top priority since 1985, eradicating polio worldwide (99.9% done!).
  • Rotary Peace Fellowships: Fully funded master's degrees/professional development certificates in peace and conflict resolution.
  • Global Grants: Large-scale international projects aligned with Areas of Focus.
  • Vocational Training Teams (VTTs): Professionals travelling to teach or learn from others.

The Rotary Foundation (TRF)

  • Our Charity Arm: Transforms donations into service projects and peace initiatives.
  • Purpose: Do good in the world, advance world understanding, goodwill, and peace.
  • Funding: Primarily from Rotarian contributions.
  • Grant Types:
    • District Grants: For local/smaller-scale projects.
    • Global Grants: For large, sustainable international projects.
    • Disaster Response Grants: For immediate relief after disasters.
    • Programs of Scale: Large, multi-year initiatives.
  • Key Fact: One of the world's most highly-rated charities (e.g., 4-star rating from Charity Navigator).

Benefits of Membership

  • Friendship & Fellowship: Connect with like-minded local and global leaders.
  • Service Opportunities: Directly impact your community and the world.
  • Personal Growth: Develop leadership, public speaking, and project management skills.
  • Networking: Expand your professional and social circles.
  • Global Understanding: Exposure to diverse cultures and international issues.
  • Fun & Engagement: Social events, travel, and rewarding experiences.

Responsibilities of a Rotarian

  • Attend Meetings: Regularly engage with your club.
  • Pay Dues: Support club, district, and Rotary International operations.
  • Participate in Service: Actively engage in club and district projects.
  • Uphold Ethics: Live by the Four-Way Test in all aspects of life.
  • Introduce Others: Help grow Rotary by inviting prospective members.
  • Be an Ambassador: Represent Rotary positively in your community.

Engaging as a Rotarian

  • Attend Club Meetings & Events: Regular presence is key.
  • Join a Committee: Contribute your skills to specific club functions or projects.
  • Lead a Project: Take ownership of a service initiative.
  • Build Relationships: Connect deeply with fellow Rotarians.
  • Leverage Your Network: Utilize professional skills and contacts for Rotary's good.

Polio Fund

Rotary International began its fight against polio in 1979. Hundreds of thousands of volunteers have supported National Immunization Days. Since 1988, nearly 3 billion children have received oral polio vaccine, and we’ve achieved a 99.9% reduction in polio cases. It may be considered the greatest humanitarian service the world has ever seen, and every Rotary member can take pride in this achievement.

Karnataka – districts

Karnataka hosts multiple Rotary districts, including: District 3192 – Bengaluru Urban & nearby District 3191 – Bengaluru suburbs District 3181 – Southern Karnataka District 3170/3160 – North Karnataka Each district has its own DG, projects, and culture.

District roles

District Governor (DG): Leads the district for a year Assistant Governor (AG): Supports a few clubs District Chairs: Lead areas like Membership, Foundation, Public Image District Secretary & Treasurer: Handle operations and finances These leaders support clubs and align district goals with RI.

3192 geographic extent

District 3192 spans parts of Bengaluru Urban, Bengaluru Rural, Ramanagara, and neighboring regions. It includes clubs in both metro and semi-urban/rural areas, promoting inclusive service.

Club roles

Key club leadership roles: President: Club head for the year Secretary: Communication and reporting Treasurer: Manages funds Club Admin, Membership, Foundation Chairs Youth, Vocational, Community, International Directors Teamwork is essential for a vibrant club.

Other roles you will encounter

IPP (Immediate Past President) Sergeant-at-Arms Public Image Chair Bulletin Editor Club Trainer These support roles ensure continuity and efficiency.

Fellowship Groups

Rotary Fellowships are groups of members who share common interests. Examples: Cycling, Golfing, Photography, Wine, Hiking, Scouting They promote international friendships beyond clubs.

Action Groups

Rotarian Action Groups (RAGs) focus on service areas like: Disaster Relief Health and Disease Prevention Peacebuilding They are open to experts and passionate members from all over the world.

Satellite club

A Satellite Club is a smaller group sponsored by a Rotary Club. They: Operate under the main club Can meet at different times/locations Are ideal for new or niche groups They promote flexibility and growth.

RCC - Rotary Community Corps

RCCs are community-based groups sponsored by Rotary Clubs. Key features: Members are non-Rotarians Empower communities to take charge of development Common in rural/service-heavy areas

Rotaract

Rotaract Clubs are for ages 18 and above. They: Build leadership and professional skills Work independently or alongside Rotary Clubs Pay lower dues than Rotary Rotaractors are considered partners in service.

Interact

Interact Clubs are for school students aged 12 to 18. They: Focus on leadership, community service Are sponsored by Rotary Clubs Hold their own meetings and activities Great way to build early leadership.

Installation

The Installation Ceremony is when the new club board formally takes charge. It includes: Formal oath-taking Recognition of past leaders Plans for the new year It’s often a celebratory public event.

GOV - Governor's Official Visit

Once a year, the DG visits each club. The visit includes: Review of club progress Meeting with board and members Encouragement and recognition It is a formal and motivational moment in the Rotary year.

Board meetings

Held monthly by the club leadership team to: Review plans Approve budgets Track progress Open, structured communication keeps clubs strong.

Club meetings

Usually weekly or bi-weekly. They: Share updates Include speakers Encourage fellowship Can be in-person, hybrid or online.
